## The whole plan

Five bug fixes + one additive set, smallest first. Each is independently
shippable.

| # | Symptom | Fix | Size |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | dock SPEAKS "face neutral" aloud | strip every `[face:]`, not just a leading one | tests green incl. delta-by-delta; **not heard live** (net −6 lines) |
| 2 | "shows crying when it should be angry" | sweat drop ≠ tear drop | ✅ **VERIFIED — photographed on the dock, build 38** |
| 3 | "mood hangs for an hour" | `userVoice` stops killing the decay timer | both halves mutation-tested; **not lived with** |
| 4 | **"why are you sad?" → incoherent** | every mood carries a **reason**; ship it to the LLM — and **name whose face** | built, tested; **live check pending** |
| 5 | face out of sync with speech | mood rides the TTS queue, like voice already does | **PARKED** — real, but the least-felt and the biggest lift |
| 6 | state is invisible (speaking, disconnected) | **effects** — copy the listening glow's playbook | speaking + disconnect BUILT; startle parked |

1–3 are bug fixes with no design content. 4 is the highest-value change here
(§Fix 4) and is nearly free. 5 is the only one with a real decision in it.
6 is **additive UX, not a bug fix** — nothing is broken; the dock is just mute
about things it already knows.

**1–4 + 6 are built and on the dock (build 47). 5 is parked.**

**The insight that reorders everything:** the face being *wrong* is survivable —
the face being **inexplicable** is not. A dock that says *"I look concerned
because you sounded worried"* is coherent even when the expression is a bad call.
A dock that can't say why is broken no matter how good its moods are. Fix 4 buys
coherence independent of whether 2/3/5 ever land.

## The evidence base

Everything below is grounded in real data, not reasoning:

- **805 inline `[face:]` tags** in the persisted sessions:
  `neutral` 349, `curious` 219, `happy` 173, `concerned` 64. **`sad` and `angry`:
  zero. Never sent, not once.**
- **`set_face` tool calls** tell a different story: `happy` 75, `curious` 55,
  `neutral` 23, `sad` 19, `concerned` 12, `excited` 5, `love` 4, `surprised` 3.
- **`turn-75cb44ad`**: 24 `[face:neutral]` tags spoken aloud; face fired 4.0s
  **before** audio started; audio ran **95s** after that.

Two things follow, and both corrected an earlier draft of this doc:

1. **`concerned` is the ONLY negative mood the dock ever wears** on the inline
   path — and it renders as a teardrop (§2). Every negative-looking face you have
   ever seen was `concerned` drawing a tear. There is no sad-vs-angry mixup to
   explain: neither is ever sent.
2. **The inline path uses 4 moods; the tool path uses 8.** Same model, same enum.
   The prompt's inline instruction is quietly narrowing the emotional range —
   which is a far better explanation for "emotions aren't rich enough" than the
   `sleepy`/`wink` enum gap I first blamed. See §Later.

## Fix 1 — the dock speaks the tag aloud (`session.ts`)

`turn-75cb44ad`, verbatim:

```
One! Starting again!
[face:neutral] Two! A familiar friend.
[face:neutral] Three! The magic number.
… ×24
```

The first tag is stripped. The other **24 are spoken**. The dock said "face
neutral" 24 times across a 95-second reply.

**Why:** the parser assumes `[face:]` appears **once, at position 0**. The field
comments say so: `#moodStepChecked` — *"this step's **leading** text has been
classified"*; `#moodStripLen` — *"chars to strip"*. One strip site, at the front.

The prompt says *"Start EVERY reply's text with a mood tag"*. Gemini applied that
per **line** — a fair reading. Every tag after the first sits at a non-zero
offset, so the stripper never sees it and it flows to TTS.

`#moodApplied` ("first tag wins") worked correctly — the *face* was set once. The
bug is that setting and stripping are coupled to the same leading-position check.
**They shouldn't be.**

**Fix (SHIPPED):** strip every `[face:NAME]` occurrence; keep the leading-only
check for *setting* the face (`#moodApplied`, first wins). `#moodHeldRaw` (which
stops `[thinks` causing dead air) is untouched.

**It made the code smaller.** `#filterMood` was stateful because it tracked a
*prefix offset* (`#moodStripLen`) across each step's cumulative text — with a
global, idempotent `stripMoodTag`, there's no offset to carry. The field and both
its resets are **deleted**; setting and stripping are now cleanly separate
concerns in one function instead of one interleaved offset dance.

A fix that deletes state is the right shape. If Fix 1 had *added* bookkeeping,
that would have been the signal to think again.

Tighten the prompt too (*"exactly one tag, at the very start"*) — but the parser
must be robust regardless. **A prompt is not a guarantee.**

Worst on long replies, which is why it surfaced now.

## Fix 2 — the teardrop (`FacePrimitives.kt:583`)

`concerned` and `sad` draw the **same teardrop** — same `drawTear`, same
`eyeBright` colour; only position and size differ:

```kotlin
ACCENT_TEAR  -> drawTear(c, eyeR * 0.35f, palette.eyeBright.copy(alpha = 0.85f))  // sad
ACCENT_SWEAT -> drawTear(c, eyeR * 0.4f,  palette.eyeBright.copy(alpha = 0.9f))   // concerned
```

Combined with the tag counts: `concerned` is the dock's **only** negative
expression in practice, fired on ~8% of turns, and it renders as crying. The LLM
was right every time; the renderer lied.

**Fix:** `drawSweatBead` — round, high beside the temple, clear of the eye.
Deliberately the visual opposite of the tear's pointed, falling drop.

**✅ VERIFIED by photograph** (build 38, via the harness): `concerned` shows a
small round bead floating clear of the eye; `sad` shows a large pointed teardrop
hanging off it. Unmistakably different. **The first attempt was NOT** — it
rendered as a slightly smaller tear (measured bounds ratio **0.63** vs the tear's
0.69: *more* pointed, the opposite of the intent) while its comment claimed "the
visual opposite: round." I had described the shape I meant and never looked at
the one I made. The rewrite (four arcs, circle-from-beziers) measures **1.16**.
**Render geometry before believing it — the intent is not visible in the math.**

**NEW, found in that same screenshot:** `concerned`'s **eyebrows are sharply
angled down-and-inward** — the classic *angry* brow. The bead now reads worried;
the brows still read annoyed. Invisible from the code (it's one number,
`browInnerY`, in the shape table) and obvious in a picture. Likely a contributor
to "the emotions feel wrong". One-number fix; not yet made.

**Scope, stated honestly: this reaches 2 of 7 faces.** Only `AuroraFace` and
`PuppyFace` call `drawAccent`; `Vader`, `Robot`, `Ghost`, `Owl`, `Dragon` render
no accents at all. Those five never *cried* (no tear either), so this is not a
regression — but the "concerned must never read as crying" goal is only met on
the **default face** + Puppy. `RobotFace.kt:143` separately collapses `Sad` and
`Concerned` into one identical eye shape; unrelated to this fix, worth knowing.

## Fix 3 — the hour-long hang (`FaceController.kt:124`)

```kotlin
fun userVoice(active: Boolean) {
    if (active) wakeUp()          // ← the entire bug
    _speaker.value = if (active) Speaker.User else Speaker.Silent
}
```

`wakeUp()` cancels the sleepy timer. `userVoice` fires on **every VAD edge**,
including ambient room noise. Nothing rearms it:

> turn ends on `Curious` → timer armed → 30s later someone coughs → timer
> cancelled **forever** → `Curious` pinned until the next turn.

`curious` is the #2 most-sent tag (219) — exactly the mood you reported hanging.

**Fix:** `wakeUp()` conflates **two independent jobs** — cancel the timer, and
un-Sleepy the face. This path wants only the second.

```kotlin
fun userVoice(active: Boolean) {
    if (active && _expression.value == FaceExpression.Sleepy) {
        _expression.value = FaceExpression.Neutral   // wake the face…
    }                                                // …but leave the timer armed
    _speaker.value = if (active) Speaker.User else Speaker.Silent
}
```

**The first cut of this fix just deleted the `wakeUp()` call — and that was a
regression, caught only by adversarial review after it had already shipped to the
dock as build 35.** It fixed the hang and broke waking: VAD is **local**
(`PerceptionPipeline`, on-device audio), but `listen()` is **remote-only**
(`renderConvMode` ← `convMode` ← an inbound station frame, written at exactly one
site: `RemoteBrain.kt:408`). With the station down or slow, the dock sat visibly
**asleep while someone talked to it**. The code comment asserting "renderConvMode
covers this" was simply wrong.

Two tests now pin **both halves** — the timer survives ambient noise, *and* a
sleepy face wakes on speech with no station. Neither alone is sufficient; the
naive fix passed the first and failed the second.

**The lesson:** `wakeUp()`'s name described one job while its body did two.
Deleting a call to it deleted both. Before removing a call, read what the callee
actually does — not what its name implies.

## Fix 4 — every mood carries a reason

### First: TWO faces, one vocabulary (the naming bug underneath)

Before any of this, a hazard that Fix 4 would otherwise make worse. **There are
two completely different faces in this system and the code barely distinguishes
them:**

| | The HUMAN's face | The ROBOT's face |
|---|---|---|
| what | a person in the camera | pixels on the phone screen |
| source | MLKit FER | LLM / dev panel / mirroring |
| type | `UserEmotion.Kind` | `FaceExpression` |
| values | `Neutral, Happy, Sad, Surprised, Sleepy, Angry` | `Neutral, Happy, Sad, Surprised, Sleepy, …` |
| wire | `perceive.emotion` | *(not sent)* |
| to the LLM | `"they appear sad"` | `"Current face: happy"` |

**The two enums share member names.** `PerceptionWiring.kt:246-251` maps one onto
the other with a literal identity function:

```kotlin
PerceptionEvent.UserEmotion.Kind.Happy -> FaceExpression.Happy   // human → robot
```

So `Happy` means *"the human looks happy"* or *"the robot looks happy"* depending
purely on **which enum you imported**. Nothing in the name tells you. In a
codebase where an LLM reads these strings, that is a live hazard, not a style nit.

The LLM already gets **both in one sentence**, disambiguated only by prose:

```
Current face: happy. You can see someone (toward your left); they appear sad.
```

*"Current face"* = the robot. *"they appear"* = the human. One word apart, and
it's the model's job to keep them straight. **This is a plausible cause of
"why are you sad?" getting an incoherent answer** — asked about its own face, the
dock may answer about yours. Two moods are in scope, both called a "face", and
the wire field is a bare `emotion` with no owner.

### The rule: every emotion field names its SUBJECT

Not a rename for tidiness — a rule that makes the confusion **unrepresentable**:

| Concept | Name |
|---|---|
| human, from camera | `userEmotion` / `perceivedEmotion` |
| robot, on screen | `dockMood` / `expression` |

Applied at the three places it matters:

1. **Wire (`perceive` frame):** `emotion` → **`userEmotion`**. Do this **now** —
   grep says **no server-side consumer reads it yet** (`grounding.ts` /
   `summarizer.ts`: zero hits). It is free today and expensive the moment the
   summarizer starts fusing it.
2. **LLM context:** make the subject explicit and unmissable —
   `Your face: happy (because …). The person you see appears sad.`
   Never two bare "face"s in one string.
3. **`PerceptionSnapshot.emotion`** → `userEmotion`. It is *only* ever the human
   (`onEmotion` is fed solely by `UserEmotion`), so the name should say so.

Renaming the *enums* (`FaceExpression` → `DockMood`) is the honest fix but a
wide diff. **Not now.** Fields and LLM-facing strings first — that's where the
confusion actually reaches the model.

### The bug: it knows WHAT, never WHY

Ask the dock *"why do you look sad?"* and it cannot answer coherently. It will
confabulate — because it genuinely does not know.

**Correcting an earlier claim in this doc:** a previous draft said the LLM is
"flying blind" about its own face. **That was wrong.** `DockTools.kt:111` already
ships this with every turn:

```kotlin
sb.append("Current face: ${face.expression.value.name.lowercase()}.")
```

So the LLM *does* know it's wearing `curious`. It has never known **why**. The
gap is narrower than claimed — and more interesting: the dock is told a fact
about itself with no provenance, so when asked about it, it invents one.

### Why this outranks getting the mood right

The face being *wrong* is survivable. The face being **inexplicable** is not.

> *"I look concerned because you sounded worried about the deadline."*

is coherent even if `concerned` was a bad call. Whereas:

> *"I'm sad because… I sense a heaviness in our conversation."*

is the dock making something up about its own internal state — worse than a wrong
face, because it's a wrong face **plus** a confabulated inner life. Fix 4 buys
coherence **independent of** whether the moods themselves ever improve.

### The reason does not need to be meaningful — only true

This is the whole design constraint, and it's liberating:

| Setter | Reason |
|---|---|
| inline `[face:]` / `set_face` | `"my reply was reassuring"` (the LLM's own words) |
| dev panel button | `"someone pressed the curious button on the dev panel"` |
| camera mirroring | `"you looked happy"` |
| wink keyword | `"you made a joke"` |
| sleepy timer | `"nobody's been around for a while"` |
| face-style default | `"it's how I start up"` |

*"Someone pressed a button"* is a **great** reason. It's honest, it's coherent,
and it lets the dock say *"no idea, someone set it from the dev panel"* — which
is exactly right and impossible today. **Dumb-but-true beats profound-but-invented.**

### As built (2026-07-15)

`FaceController.MoodReason(why, source, atMs)` — a flow beside `_expression`,
written only through `writeExpression(e, why, source)` so a mood and its reason
**can never disagree**. All seven writers name their own:

| source | why |
|---|---|
| `llm` | the model's own `set_face(reason:)` — its real account |
| `mirror` | *"I'm mirroring the expression I see on your face"* |
| `timer` | *"nothing's happened for a while, so I got sleepy"* |
| `wake` / `voice` | *"I woke up when I heard someone talking"* |
| `wink` | *"I winked — you made a joke"* (restores the prior mood **and its reason**) |
| `boot` | *"I just started up"* |

The per-turn line now names the subject and the cause:

```
YOUR face (the robot's, on your screen): angry — because I'm mirroring the
expression I see on your face. (That's THEIR feeling copied onto you by the
camera, not your own — say so plainly if asked.) You can see guru; they appear angry.
```

**Two things the tests caught that I'd have shipped wrong:**

1. `RemoteBrainTest` asserted `contains("Current face:")` — the exact string this
   replaces. The test was right; the contract moved. It now asserts the *new*
   contract (`YOUR face` + `because`), which is what should have been pinned all
   along.
2. My first mirror test failed: an explicit mood **locks out the camera for
   2.5s** (`passiveLockUntilMs`), so mirroring right after `setExpression` is
   correctly ignored. My test was wrong, not the code — now both behaviours are
   pinned (the lock holds; once lapsed, the mirror writes and is marked borrowed).

### Where the state lives

**On the phone, beside the expression** — the arbiter and the reason must be the
same writer, or they drift.

Extend the value, don't add a parallel field:

```kotlin
data class Mood(
    val value: FaceExpression,
    val reason: String,        // required — no default, forces every setter to say why
    val source: String,        // "llm" | "dev" | "mirror" | "reflex" | "timer"
)
private val _mood = MutableStateFlow(Mood(FaceExpression.Neutral, "just started up", "timer"))
```

`reason` is **required, no default**. That's the point: the compiler makes every
setter answer "why". There are only **six call sites** in the whole app —
`DockTools.kt:213`, `:200` (wink), `PerceptionWiring.kt:201`, `:255`,
`DevPanel.kt:225`, `:228` — so this is genuinely cheap, and it stays cheap
because a new setter *cannot* be added without a reason.

Keep `source` separate from `reason`: `source` is for filtering (don't feed a
mirrored mood back to the LLM as intent), `reason` is for speaking.

### How the LLM gets it

`currentContext()` already exists, already ships every turn, and the face line is
already the first thing in it. It becomes one string longer — **and names its
subject** (§the rule above):

```
Your face: concerned (because you sounded worried). The person you see appears worried.
```

That's the entire integration for Fix 4. No new frame, no new protocol, no
station change.

### Does mood go on the perceive stream? Not yet — and NOT as "emotion"

Two separate questions, and they have different answers.

**Is it a new stream?** No. If mood is ever reported, it rides the **existing**
`perceive` frame — `PerceiveForwarder` already folds emotion/gesture/identity
into one coherent "what the dock perceives now" message, with transition/
heartbeat/dedup hysteresis already built and tuned. Mood is one more folded
field. A second stream would need its own hysteresis and could arrive out of
order with the first. **Never a new stream.**

**Should it go there now?** **No.** Fix 4's complaint — *"ask it why it's sad and
it's incoherent"* — is fully solved by `currentContext()`, which already ships
every turn. Putting mood on the wire serves *other* consumers (the summarizer,
traces, the console), and none of them are asking yet. See §Later item 2.

**When it does happen, the field names are non-negotiable:**

```jsonc
{
  "userEmotion": { "kind": "sad", "confidence": 0.8 },   // the HUMAN (renamed)
  "dockMood":    { "value": "concerned", "reason": "…", "source": "llm" }  // the ROBOT
}
```

Never a bare `emotion`, never two things called `face`. If both the human's
emotion and the robot's mood land in the same frame — which is exactly what a
"coherent perception snapshot" means — **subject-naming stops being style and
becomes correctness.** A summarizer fusing `emotion: sad` + `mood: sad` without
knowing whose is which will produce confident nonsense, and it will be an LLM
doing the fusing, so the failure will be fluent and hard to spot.

That's the real reason the rename lands in Fix 4 rather than waiting: **Fix 4 is
what makes the robot's mood a first-class fact.** The moment two moods are
first-class, they must be nameable apart.

### Why `set_face` should take the reason

**Yes — and it's the best part of this.** Add an optional `reason` to
`setFaceSchema`:

```
set_face(expression: "concerned", reason: "you said the deploy failed")
```

The LLM is the one setter that *knows* its own reason, and today that reason is
thrown away at the exact moment it exists. Capturing it costs one schema field.
Next turn, its own stated reason comes back in `currentContext()` — so it answers
"why do you look concerned?" from **its own record**, not by re-inventing one.

Inline `[face:]` tags can't carry a reason (they're one token, and keeping them
cheap is their whole purpose — the RCA measured the separate `set_face` step at a
full serial ttft). Fall back to a generic-but-true reason there:
`"it matched what I was saying"`. Honest, and it doesn't cost latency.

### Test

Ask *"why do you look sad?"* right after a dev-panel force-set. A coherent
answer names the button press. That's the bar — not profundity, **honesty**.

## Fix 5 — sync the face to the audio, not the parse

### The problem, measured

`turn-75cb44ad`:

| event | time | Δ |
|---|---|---|
| text done streaming (**face fired here**) | `…005621` | — |
| speech **starts** | `…009588` | **+4.0s** |
| speech **ends** | `…105141` | **+99.5s** |

`fireFace` runs at parse time, fire-and-forget — deliberately: *"the graphic is
best-effort UX and must never block a reply."* But `DockTts` is a **queue**:
sentences *"synthesize near-simultaneously but play sequentially"*, tracked
against `playbackEndsAt`.

So the face went neutral **4 seconds before the dock said a word**, then held
across **95 seconds** of speech. On a 1–2 sentence reply the skew is invisible.
It scales with reply length.

### Why this killed the earlier design

An earlier draft proposed a 12s TTL: *"covers a typical reply (3–8s)."* **That
was measured against the wrong clock** — text-stream time, not audio time. On
this turn the audio ran 95s, so a 12s TTL would have reset the face to Neutral
~83 seconds before the dock stopped talking: mood dying mid-sentence while it
cheerfully counts to 25.

**Decay-from-parse is not a smaller version of the right fix. It's wrong.** Fix 5
replaces it.

### The mechanism already exists

`DockTts.kt:42-45`, on per-face voice:

> *"setPitch/setSpeechRate affect **SUBSEQUENTLY queued utterances**, so a
> mid-stream face swap takes effect from the next sentence — **the desired
> behaviour**."*

**Voice is already synchronized to the audio queue.** The face just doesn't use
the same pattern. This is one existing mechanism extended to a second consumer,
not new machinery.

### The seam is already there

- `speak` frames are already **per-sentence**, already carry `turnId`
  (`RemoteBrain.onSpeak`). A `mood` field rides along naturally.
- `enqueueSentence(text: String)` is text-only today → `(text, mood?)`.
- `SpeakingEdgeGate` already computes the real speech-ended edge (it exists
  because inter-sentence gaps falsely read as "stopped speaking") — that's the
  correct decay anchor, already built.

### The change

1. **Station:** don't `fireFace` at parse. Attach the mood to the `speak` frame
   for the sentence it preceded.
2. **Phone:** `enqueueSentence(text, mood)` applies the mood as that utterance
   starts — exactly where voice already lands.
3. **Decay:** anchor to `SpeakingEdgeGate`'s speech-end, not to a parse
   timestamp. A mood lives until its reply's **audio** finishes, then decays.

~30 lines. No priority lattice, no TTL table, no new protocol.

### The bonus

This reframes Fix 1's 24 tags. On a per-line-tagged reply they aren't just noise
to strip — they're **positional information**: which mood belongs to which
sentence. Today's design throws that away, keeps the first, speaks the rest.
Fix 5 makes per-line tags *work*: the dock could shift expression as it counts.
The model was already trying to do the right thing.

**Fix 1 must still strip them all** — Fix 5 is what makes them meaningful, and
it's opt-in per sentence. Ship 1 first regardless.

Fix 5 also sharpens Fix 4's reasons: a speech-anchored mood knows the sentence it
belongs to, so its reason can be *"I was saying: Twelve! A dozen of delight."* —
provenance for free.

## Fix 6 — effects: make state legible without words

Separate from 1–5: those fix bugs, **this is additive UX**. Nothing here is
broken; the dock is just mute about things it already knows.

### The template: why the listening glow works

`ListeningGlow.kt` is the model to copy, and it's worth naming *why* it's good:

- **a host overlay, not per-face** — works for Aurora/Vader/Robot/… for free, and
  a new face gets it without authoring anything
- **breathing, not blinking** — a 1.6s pulse reads as *alive*; a blink reads as an
  *alarm*
- **gated with a 450ms fade** — never pops in or out
- **tinted by the active face's accent** — belongs to whatever face is showing
- **paired with a beep + haptic** — the cue policy in `PerceptionWiring` is already
  careful about *when* it's allowed to fire

It also carries a scar worth heeding (from its own comments): a colour tween that
chased a per-frame pulse target **never caught up**, and the rim settled at ~0.09
alpha — invisible. **Gate the effect ON/OFF with the tween; apply the pulse
directly.** Any new effect that animates a moving target risks the same bug.

### The signals — all four already exist as state

Nothing needs new plumbing. This is a **rendering** gap, not a data gap:

| Effect | Signal | Where it lives | Today |
|---|---|---|---|
| speaking | `FaceState.Speaking` | `FaceController.state` | state exists, **no effect** |
| station disconnected | `stationLink.connected` | `DockScreen.kt:346` | collected, **text only** |
| body disconnected | *(none on phone)* | station-side | **not plumbed** — see below |
| sudden sound | `wiring.audioLevel` (RMS ~10 Hz) | `DockScreen.kt:531` | drives a meter only |

### Proposed effects

**1. Speaking — the counterpart to listening.** The obvious gap: the dock glows
when *you* talk and shows nothing when *it* does. Make it the deliberate
**inverse** of listening — listening pulls attention *in* (rim glow, breathing,
calm); speaking pushes *out* (a soft centre-out bloom on sentence starts, keyed
off the same per-sentence `speak` frames Fix 5 uses). **Not** a rim pulse: two
rim effects would be confusable, and these are the two states that most need to
be told apart at a glance.

**2. Station disconnected — the most important one.** Right now a disconnected
dock **looks completely normal** and just silently stops responding. That's the
worst failure mode in the app: indistinguishable from "ignoring me". Give it a
**desaturation + slow dim** — the face drains of colour and the eyes go dull. It
reads instantly as *"something's wrong with it"* rather than *"it's mad at me"*,
and it degrades gracefully: partial desaturation during a reconnect blip is
honest, not alarming. Deliberately **unlike** every other effect: nothing else
should ever drain colour.

**3. Sudden sound (RMS) — a startle.** `audioLevel` is already there at ~10 Hz.
A sharp jump (not absolute level — the **delta**) → a quick reflex: eyes widen, a
tiny gaze snap, settling back over ~600ms. This is the cheapest charm in the
whole doc and the most likely to make it feel alive. **Must be rate-limited hard**
(≤1 per ~3s) or a noisy room turns the dock into a twitching mess. Suppress
entirely while `Speaking` — it would fire on the dock's own voice (the AEC path
already has this exact problem, see `BargeIn`).

**4. Body disconnected — needs plumbing first.** The phone has no body-status
signal (`RemoteBrain` has none; the body digest is station-side). Real gap, but
it's a **wiring task before an effect task**. The honest cue once it exists: the
face is fine, something *below* is not — the body is a separate creature from the
face. Suggest deferring until 1–3 land.

### The rule that keeps this from becoming noise

**Each effect owns a distinct visual channel** — otherwise they stack into a
Christmas tree during a normal turn:

| Effect | Channel |
|---|---|
| listening | rim glow, breathing |
| speaking | centre bloom, per sentence |
| disconnected | **saturation** (nothing else touches it) |
| startle | eye geometry + gaze, brief |
| mood | eye/brow shape + accent (Fix 2–5) |

Listening + speaking are **mutually exclusive** by state. Disconnected and
startle can legitimately coexist with anything — which is exactly why they must
not share a channel with each other or with mood.

### Sequencing

**3 → 2 → 1 → 4.** The startle is the smallest and the most delightful; the
disconnect cue prevents the worst misread; speaking is the biggest lift (it
wants Fix 5's per-sentence frames to be good, so it naturally lands after);
body needs plumbing.

None of these block 1–5. **They're also the only items here that need a human to
judge** — "does it feel alive?" is not a unit test. Per
`verify-user-perceivable-outcomes.md`: sign off in the user's modality, on the
real device, or don't sign off.

## Explicitly NOT doing

Considered and rejected as overengineering:

- ~~5-source priority lattice with per-source TTLs~~ — two rules cover it. The
  existing `passiveLockUntilMs` (2.5s) is a hack that *works*; replacing a
  working hack with a model is pure cost.
- ~~ambient mood drift / "gets bored"~~ — the dock already blinks, breathes, and
  micro-saccades (`rememberFaceFrame`). It does not look dead. "Bored" is
  plausibly a *symptom of the hang* (Fix 3), not a missing feature.
- ~~state-dependent baseline table~~ — Neutral is a fine baseline; the sleepy
  timer covers the one interesting case.
- ~~level-triggered FER~~ — only matters if mirroring is load-bearing. After
  Fix 3, it probably isn't.
- ~~decay ticker from parse time~~ — **wrong clock** (§Fix 4).

## Later — genuinely separate

1. **The inline path is emotionally narrower than the tool path** (4 moods vs 8,
   from the same model + enum). The best lead on "emotions aren't rich enough",
   and it's a **prompt** issue, not a renderer one. Worth a look at why
   `FACE_INLINE` collapses the range.
2. **Report `dockMood` on the `perceive` stream.** Fix 4 gives the *LLM* the
   reason via `currentContext()`. The rest of the system still can't see the
   robot's face: the summarizer fuses vision/speech/identity/userEmotion/
   bodymotion but not the dock's own affect, and traces record what was
   *requested*, not what was *shown*. Small (fold `dockMood` into the frame
   `PerceiveForwarder` already sends — **not a new stream**), but it's a
   **capability**, not a bug fix; Fix 4 covers the coherence complaint alone.
   **Precondition:** the §Fix 4 rename. Landing `dockMood` next to a bare
   `emotion` is how the summarizer starts confidently confusing the human's mood
   with the robot's — fluently, and in an LLM, so nobody catches it.
3. **`sleepy` + `wink` unreachable by the LLM** (11 phone expressions, 9 in
   `FACES`). Leave it — both are system-owned (Sleepy is a timer, Wink a reflex).
   The enums *should* differ. Just document it.
4. **Palm / face-arrival produce no face feedback** — the dock doesn't visibly
   notice you. A `wink()` on palm-ack would be charming. Cheap, but a feature.

## Test plan

Three tiers. **Automated tests prove the mechanism; only the live tier proves the
experience** (`verify-user-perceivable-outcomes.md`: voice-stop passed 3× in
state-level tests and failed every human try).

### Tier 1 — automated (must stay green)

**Tier 1 is the FLOOR, not a sign-off.** It proves logic in a JVM/Node process.
It says nothing about the app: no APK here was ever built from this code, and no
device was ever connected (`adb devices` = empty). Do not read a green Tier 1 as
"it works".

**Fix 2 has NO Tier 1 coverage and cannot have any meaningful coverage.** It is a
drawing change; asserting on `drawPath` geometry would test the implementation,
not "does it read as crying". `compileDebugKotlin` passing means it *compiles* —
nothing more. **A broken-looking bead passes every test in this table.** Fix 2 is

**The Fix 3 test was mutation-verified**: reintroducing `if (active) wakeUp()`
made it fail ("22 tests completed, 1 failed"), then pass again on revert. A
regression test that never fails on the bug is decoration. Do this for every fix
here — the bugs are all *absence* bugs (a timer that doesn't fire, a tag that
isn't stripped), and absence is exactly what a green test hides.

Fix 2 (teardrop) has **no unit test**: asserting on `drawPath` geometry would test
the implementation, not "does it read as crying". It's a Tier 3 item.

### Tier 2 — on-device, mechanically checkable

Sideload (`./gradlew :app:installDebug`), then drive turns over adb:

```bash
adb shell am broadcast -a dev.orbit.dock.debug.SAY -e text 'count to twenty five, one number per sentence'
```

- **Fix 1:** listen. Zero "face neutral" utterances. This is the exact
  `turn-75cb44ad` repro — a long reply is where the bug lives.
- **Fix 3:** end a turn on a non-neutral mood, make ambient noise (talk near the
  dock without addressing it), wait 90s → face reaches Sleepy. Before the fix it
  stayed pinned. **Needs real ambient noise** — the bug rides the VAD edge, and
  an injected event proves the mechanism, not the path.

### Tier 3 — live, human-judged (the only sign-off that counts)

- **Fix 2 (the real test):** ask something that draws `concerned` (the only
  negative mood the brain sends — e.g. tell it something worrying). **Does it read
  as worried, or as crying?** One human look settles it; no assertion can.
- **Fix 4 (when built):** force a mood from the dev panel, ask *"why do you look
  sad?"* → the answer names the button press.
- **Fix 4 subject confusion — the test that matters most:** set the two moods in
  **opposition** (dock forced `happy`, a visibly sad person in frame), then ask
  *"how are you feeling?"* and *"how do I look?"*. Both answers the right way
  round, or the rename didn't work.
- **Fix 6:** "does it feel alive / do I notice it's disconnected?" — judgement
  only.

Per `embodied-control-learnings.md`: judge the **aggregate**, not a single run.
These are flaky-by-nature paths (VAD, camera, TTS timing) — one good run is not
a pass.

## Test notes

**Correcting an earlier claim:** this doc said `gazeAndExpressionStoreLatest`
"encodes the bug" by asserting `Curious` sticks. **Wrong** — it only checks a
setter stores its value; no timer is involved, and it passes fine after Fix 3.

The real gap was subtler and more interesting. `userVoiceTogglesSpeakerOnly`
already existed, already asserted "speaker **only**" — and checked only `state`,
never the **timer** `wakeUp()` was silently destroying. **The test's name stated
the correct contract; its body didn't enforce it.** That's how the bug lived
under a green suite for so long. Fix 3's new test enforces the name.

And the broader claim held: no test advanced virtual time, so the 90s timer was
never exercised at all.

- Fix 1: a reply with N tags speaks zero of them; the face still sets once
  (regression: the `turn-75cb44ad` shape — tags at non-zero offsets)
- Fix 2: sweat and tear accents don't render identically
- Fix 3: `userVoice(true)` does not cancel a pending sleepy transition
- Fix 4: every setter records a reason; `currentContext()` carries it. The real
  test is a **live conversation**: force a mood from the dev panel, ask "why do
  you look sad?", and check the answer names the button. Per
  `verify-user-perceivable-outcomes.md` — state-level tests prove the mechanism,
  not the experience. This one must be judged by talking to it.
- Fix 4 (subject confusion), the test that matters most: **set the two moods in
  opposition** — force the dock to `happy` from the dev panel while a visibly
  `sad` person is in frame — then ask *"how are you feeling?"* and *"how do I
  look?"*. Getting the two answers **the right way round** is the whole point.
  Today's `"Current face: happy … they appear sad"` is one prose cue away from
  swapping them, and nothing else in the system would notice.
- Fix 5: mood applies at its sentence's playback, not at parse; decay anchors to
  speech-end. Needs a virtual clock — inject `nowMs` into `FaceController` as
  `PerceptionWiring` already does for `PresenceGate` (the pattern exists).

## Why this doc keeps shrinking

Draft 1: a 5-source priority lattice, per-source TTLs, ambient drift, a
state-dependent baseline, and a mood-reporting protocol. ~400 lines of model to
fix what turned out to be **two one-line bugs** — which it would have buried.

Draft 2 cut that, but kept a 12s decay TTL. A real trace then showed the TTL was
measured against the **text clock** while the audio ran 25× longer. The
simplification was right; one surviving number was still wrong.

Draft 3 added Fix 4 and reordered around it. "The face should be able to explain
itself" sounds like a feature request; it's the cheapest fix here (~15 lines,
six call sites, one existing string) and the only one that helps **even when
every other fix fails**. A wrong-but-honest face is fine. An inexplicable face is
broken.

Two lessons worth keeping:

1. **The traces answered in one query what the code review argued about for two
   rounds.** 805 tags said `sad`/`angry` are never sent. One turn said the clocks
   are 95 seconds apart. Neither is visible from reading code.
2. **I claimed the LLM was "flying blind" about its own face. It wasn't** —
   `DockTools.kt:111` had been shipping `Current face: …` all along. I'd grepped
   the station's prompt and missed that the *phone* injects it. The real gap was
   one word narrower (`what` vs `why`) and the fix an order of magnitude smaller
   than the "capability" I'd filed. **Grep the whole path before naming a gap.**
