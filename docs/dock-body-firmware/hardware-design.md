# dock body — hardware design (as-built)

The **dock body** is the ESP32 servo actuator of a node-dock: a small posable
robot (neck / foot / two arms) that the brain drives over the station link. This
doc is the **physical build** — boards, power, wiring, antenna, servos. The
software that runs on it is [firmware-design.md](firmware-design.md).

## 1. Boards — two MCU options, one firmware

The body firmware builds for **two interchangeable MCU boards** from one source
tree (see firmware-design.md §"Dual-chip build"). They differ physically:

| | XIAO ESP32-S3 | ESP32-C3 SuperMini |
|---|---|---|
| Core | Xtensa LX7, dual-core | RISC-V, single-core |
| Flash | 8 MB | 4 MB |
| Antenna | **u.FL external** (connector on-board) | **PCB trace** (etched, no connector) |
| Servo PWM peripheral | MCPWM | LEDC (C3 has no MCPWM) |
| OTA target `kind` | `dock-body-fw` | `dock-body-fw-c3` |
| PlatformIO env | `seeed_xiao_esp32s3` | `esp32c3_mini` |
| Wi-Fi TX power | full (20 dBm) | **capped + bulk-cap** (see §4) |

Both enumerate as native USB-Serial/JTAG (logs on `/dev/cu.usbmodem*` at
115200), both take the same servo wiring, both speak the identical station
protocol. The S3 is the original/reference board; the C3 was added as a cheaper
second option and carries extra RF caveats (§4).

## 2. Servo wiring (locked)

Four **MG90S** servos (metal-gear clones). Same GPIO numbers on both boards —
`3/4/5/6` are clean general-purpose pads on the S3 and C3 alike (none are
strapping pins on either chip). Authoritative source: `src/servo.h`.

| Joint | GPIO | signal (orange) | +5V (red) | GND (brown) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| `neck`      | GPIO 3 | → pad 3 | 5V rail | GND rail |
| `foot`      | GPIO 4 | → pad 4 | 5V rail | GND rail |
| `arm_left`  | GPIO 5 | → pad 5 | 5V rail | GND rail |
| `arm_right` | GPIO 6 | → pad 6 | 5V rail | GND rail |

- **Pulse range:** clamped to **[500, 2500] µs** in firmware (MG90S safe range);
  center/home = **1500 µs**.
- **C3 pad caution:** avoid GPIO **2 / 8 / 9** on the C3 SuperMini — they're
  strapping pins (GPIO 8 is also the onboard LED); a servo there can block
  boot/flash. The chosen 3/4/5/6 deliberately steer clear.
- **Advertised vs. wired:** the firmware initializes all four but currently only
  *advertises* `neck` + `foot` in the capability profile; `arm_left`/`arm_right`
  are held at center until mechanically wired. ⟨TODO: confirm current
  arm mechanical status.⟩

## 3. Power topology — TWO separate rails, common ground

The single hard rule from bring-up: **never power the servos off the MCU.** Four
MG90S can momentarily demand ~3 A at stall; an MCU USB rail (0.5–0.9 A) sags
below the servos' minimum, the position loop misreads, and the board brown-out
resets. So the build uses two independent rails:

**Rail A — servo power (high current)**
- Fed from **12 V → a buck converter → 5 V** ⟨TODO: confirm the exact buck part —
  bring-up notes list *MP1584 or DROK, 5 V / 5 A*.⟩
- A **1000 µF electrolytic across the 5 V servo rail** (bulk reservoir for the
  stall-current spikes).
- Drives the servos' red (+5V) and brown (GND) leads **only**.

**Rail B — the MCU**
- The ESP32 board on its **own USB / 5 V** feed, separate from the servo rail.
- The board's onboard regulator drops that to the **3V3 rail** the radio + logic
  run off.

**Critical:** tie the two grounds together so the servo signal lines (orange,
driven at 3V3 logic from the MCU) share a reference with the servo power ground.

```
  12V ──▶ buck ──▶ 5V servo rail ──┬── servo +5V (×4)
                    │              └── 1000µF ⏛
                    └── (Rail A ground) ─────────┐
                                                 ├── COMMON GND  ← tie here
  USB/5V ──▶ ESP32 ──▶ (onboard reg) ──▶ 3V3 ────┘
                    orange signal (×4) ──▶ servo GPIO 3/4/5/6
```

## 4. ESP32-C3 Wi-Fi — the TX-power / rail-sag / range trap (C3 ONLY)

The C3 SuperMini has a nasty two-sided Wi-Fi problem the S3 doesn't, solved by a
**3V3 bulk cap + a tuned TX-power cap**. Full arc is in the firmware bring-up
journal (`node-dock/body-firmware/dock_body_v0/progress.md`, §"ESP32-C3 SuperMini
Wi-Fi"); the physical fix, summarized:

**The problem.** At full TX power the C3's light 3V3 decoupling lets a transmit
burst **sag the 3V3 rail mid-frame**, corrupting the WPA handshake (fails *close*
to the AP, counter-intuitively — strong signal → radio drives harder → bigger
spike). Capping TX power to 8.5 dBm fixes the sag but is then **too weak to reach
~5 m** (50–100 % packet loss).

**The physical fix.** Add **bulk capacitance on the 3V3 rail** (NOT the 5 V input
— the sag is *downstream* of the board's 5V→3V3 regulator, on the rail the radio
actually runs off), then raise TX power back up. The cap absorbs the burst; the
higher TX buys the range. Measured at 5 m against the same AP:

| TX power (firmware `BL_C3_TX_POWER_Q`) | 3V3 cap | loss @ 5 m |
|---|---|---|
| 8.5 dBm (q=34) | none | 50–100 % (unusable) |
| 11 dBm (q=44) | none | ~15–20 % (marginal) |
| **14 dBm (q=56)** | **1× 100 µF** | **0.0 %** (120-pkt sample) ✅ |

**As-built now:** 1× 100 µF electrolytic across **3V3 ↔ GND** close to the C3, on
a breadboard, with firmware at 14 dBm.

**How much does the cap actually contribute?** Tested empirically (2026-07-04):
with the cap **removed**, at the same problem distance, at 14 dBm, the board
reconnected **cleanly 6/6 times** (forced cold handshakes via unclaim→reclaim;
RSSI −58…−60 dBm, single association each, no retries). Interpretation: **the big
lever was raising TX 8.5→14 dBm, not the cap** — at this range/RSSI there's enough
margin that the rail sag either doesn't occur or doesn't bite. The cap's specific
job is surviving the *handshake burst*, and at 14 dBm + −59 dBm that burst wasn't
stressed. So the cap is **margin, not strictly required at this range** — kept in
because it's free insurance and *becomes* necessary if you move further from the
AP (weaker RSSI → radio drives harder → bigger sag) or raise TX past 14 dBm.
(Caveat: n=6, and the test used software-triggered reboots, not power-cycles.)

**Production rules (C3 only):**
- Keep the 3V3 bulk cap as **margin** at 14 dBm; treat it as **required** if TX
  goes above 14 dBm or the dock lives further from the AP (weaker signal).
- Soldered/permanent build: **2–3× 100 µF soldered close to the C3's 3V3 pins** +
  a **0.1 µF ceramic** in parallel (handles the fast edge the electrolytics are
  too slow for). Breadboard contacts blunt the cap; solder + a bit more
  capacitance is the durable margin.
- The trace antenna has a **ceiling** — for a dock that must live far from the AP,
  prefer the **S3** (u.FL antenna: bring-up measured 60–70 % loss → **0 %** just
  by plugging the antenna in) or move the AP / add a repeater.

The S3 needs none of §4 — separate USB + better decoupling + a real antenna.

## 5. Antennas

- **XIAO ESP32-S3:** on-board **u.FL** connector, routed to an external antenna.
  **The S3 dock body must always ship with the antenna attached** — without it,
  RSSI −85…−90 dBm and 60–70 % loss; with it, −52 dBm and 0 %.
- **ESP32-C3 SuperMini:** **PCB trace antenna**, no connector. Fundamentally
  weaker; can't take an external antenna without cutting the trace and soldering
  a u.FL pigtail (delicate, not recommended). This is *why* the C3 needs the §4
  power+TX work to reach across a room.
